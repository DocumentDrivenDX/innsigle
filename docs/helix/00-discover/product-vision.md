# Product Vision

> **Product name: Innsigle** (locked). Say **INN-siggle** (rhymes with *single*).  
> From ON/Icelandic *innsigli* — document seal.  
> **House:** Azgaard. **Concepts:** signet (seal + optional signed claim); mash bill (composition, not morals).  
> **Recognition:** visual seal mark is primary; the word supports the mark.  
> **Repo slug:** `aibadge` (code name; rename optional).

## Mission Statement

Help makers put an **Innsigle**—a craft seal—on their work that declares the **mash bill of how it was made**—human, AI, tools, and blend—not a moral grade—and optionally bind that composition claim to a person or house so others can verify *who sealed it*.

## Positioning

For independent makers, small publishers, and craft-led brands who want origin to read like a **recipe of production** rather than a purity test or a detector score,
**Innsigle** is a **content signet**: a visible seal for the work’s mash bill with optional DKIM-like attribution.
Unlike Not By AI badges (human-only virtue signaling) and unlike C2PA Content Credentials (industrial media manifests),
**Innsigle** treats human, AI, and mixed work as **ingredients in one composition system**, sealed with craft pride and, when chosen, a named signer.

## Vision

Origin marks become as ordinary as a cooper’s stamp or a distiller’s grain bill on the bottle: a small, consistent **Innsigle** people learn to read by sight, proud of *how the batch was made*. Readers learn composition—not to punish AI or saint human hands. When a seal is signed, verification answers “did this maker or house bind this mash bill to this work?”—never “what did an oracle score?” Civic clarity follows craft honesty: transparency is a byproduct of makers who will put their mark on the recipe.

**North Star**: Within 24 months of public launch, people in the launch niches recognize the **Innsigle mark** as a **composition seal** (mash bill of making), and a meaningful share of sealed works offer a one-step verify path to a named maker or house.

## User Experience

Alex finishes an essay and a set of diagrams. They do not pick a scarlet letter. They record a **mash bill**: e.g. human drafting and edit; model-assisted outline; image model for one figure; final judgment human. They place the **Innsigle** in the footer—the mark reads as craft composition, same system whether the bill is all-human or heavily mixed. For the canonical essay URL they optionally **sign** the seal: hash the text, attach the bill and timestamp, sign with their key (or Azgaard house key when publishing under that brand). A curious reader opens the seal: ingredients of making, fingerprint match, who sealed it. No “% evil AI.” No purity score. Unsigned Innsigles still declare the bill; the signature is depth for those who want standing behind the batch.

## Target Market

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Who | Craft-oriented writers, designers, studios, and small publishers (solo to ~50) who already care how their work is *made*, including makers with a house brand (e.g. Azgaard-like personal mythos) |
| Pain | Public talk about origin is moralized (human good / AI bad) or industrial (pipelines, detectors); neither feels like a maker’s mark |
| Current Solution | Human-only stickers, embarrassed footnotes, platform labels, silence, or heavy provenance stacks aimed at media supply chains |
| Why They Switch | They want a **bill of materials for the work**—honest, portable, sealable—under rising disclosure pressure without adopting shame-based or enterprise-only tools |

## Key Value Propositions

| Value Proposition | Customer Benefit |
|-------------------|------------------|
| Mash-bill origin language (composition, not verdict) | Human, AI, and mixed work share one proud craft frame—ingredients, not a morality play |
| Innsigle craft signet (visible seal + optional signed claim) | Audiences see the recipe and, when present, who stands behind it (DKIM-like), without detector theater |
| Visual-first, house-friendly marks | Recognition lives in the seal mark; works on a personal domain (e.g. azgaard.net) and travels beyond a single platform |

## Success Definition

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Primary KPI | ≥ 50% of surveyed readers in two launch niches correctly describe the Innsigle mark as **how the work was made / composition**, not as “AI bad” or “human pure,” 12–24 months after public brand launch |
| Attribution depth | ≥ 25% of public Innsigles in year one expose a working verify path to a named maker or house |
| Integrity of category | Zero product claims as AI detection; public materials always separate **declared mash bill** and **signed seal** from **detected fact** |
| Adoption quality | ≥ 1,000 distinct publishing origins *or* dense use across the operator’s own properties plus a small craft cohort—final bar set in PRD |

## Why Now

Vendors and standards are hardening **file-level** provenance (Content Credentials, SynthID), platforms slap on their own labels, and rules such as EU AI Act Article 50 (from 2 August 2026) push transparency for synthetic content. Parallel “human only” badge culture proved makers want a **visible mark**—but moralized purity leaves mixed and AI-using craft without an honest, proud language. Distillers already solved a similar storytelling problem with the **mash bill**: declare the recipe, stamp the house, let quality speak. Makers of digital work still lack that seal. **Innsigle** is that craft signet for composition—with optional attributable standing—not another detector and not a full media-pipeline credential stack as the brand.
